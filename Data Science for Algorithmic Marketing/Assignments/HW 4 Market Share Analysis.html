EXPLANATION OF EXPONENTS:

  • Ownprice elasticity (Brand is volume/share vs.Brand is price): negative.
  • Crossprice elasticity (Brand is volume/share vs.Brand js price): positive for substitutes.

In your sales model, you included all 7 brand prices in the same equation for brand is log sales. That should give negative for brand is own LPRICE, positive for the others. But you found negative crossprice estimates. That is the sign that something is wrong in the sense of collinearity or misspecification:

  • Its not that your ownprice model is wrong. Actually, the negative ownprice effect is correct.
  • The issue is that the crossprice effects are also going negative, which doesnt make sense for substitutable brands. Thats the part your professor flags as a red flag for collinearity.

Ownprice elasticity should be negative:

  • Economically, raising a brands own price causes its own demand (or share) to go down.
  • So if brand As price goes up, brand As volume (or share) goes down hence the expected negative coefficient for brand As own price.

Crossprice elasticity should be positive (for substitutes):

  • If you raise a competitor brands price, demand for your brand should go up (assuming the products compete and are substitutes).
  • So if brand B raises its price, brand As volume (or share) typically should go up hence a positive coefficient on brand Bs price in brand As equation.

  tuna[[paste0("share", i)]] <- tuna[[sales_cols[i]]] / tuna$FULLCUST

# Log-transform the market shares and then log-center them.
for(i in 1:n_brands) {
  tuna[[paste0("log_share", i)]] <- log(tuna[[paste0("share", i)]])
}
# Compute the average log share across all brands for each observation
tuna$avg_log_share <- rowMeans(tuna[, paste0("log_share", 1:n_brands)])
# Create centered log share variables for each brand:
for(i in 1:n_brands) {
  tuna[[paste0("centered_log_share", i)]] <- tuna[[paste0("log_share", i)]] - tuna$avg_log_share
}
